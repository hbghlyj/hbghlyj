  2. Let $K$ be a number field, other than 𝐐. Show that ${|Δ_K|}>1$.
    Let $n$ be the degree of $K$ over 𝐐, and $2r_{2}=n-r_{1}$ be the number of complex embeddings. Then every class in the ideal class group of $K$ contains an ideal of norm not exceeding Minkowski's bound \[M_K=\left(\frac{4}{π}\right)^{r_2}\frac{n!}{n^n}\sqrt{|Δ_K|}\] Since an ideal has norm at least one, we have $1≤M_K$, so that \[ \sqrt{|Δ_K|}≥ \left(\fracπ4\right)^{r_{2}}{\frac {n^{n}}{n!}}≥ \left({\frac {π }{4}}\right)^{n/2}{\frac {n^n}{n!}}=\fracπ2\prod_{j=2}^{n-1}\frac{\left(1+\frac{1}{j}\right)^j}{\frac2{\sqrtπ}}>1 \text{since }\left(1+\frac{1}{j}\right)^j>\frac2{\sqrtπ}\text{for }j>1 \] so ${|Δ_K|}>1$.

  4. Find $\Cl(K)$, where $K=𝐐(\sqrt{65})$.
    Since $d≡1\pmod4$, $𝒪_K=𝐙[α],α=\frac{1+\sqrt{65}}2$ and $∆_K=65$. Therefore $M_K=\frac12\sqrt{65}<5$. Thus generators of $\Cl(K)$ may be found amongst the ideal prime factors of (2) and (3).
    The minimal polynomial $m(X)$ for $α$ is $X^2-X-16$.
    Over $𝐅_2$ this factors as $X(X+1)$, so by Dedekind $(2) = 𝔭_2𝔮_2$ where $𝔭_2=(2,α),𝔮_2=(2,1+α)$ have norm 2 so not principal [$N(a+bα)=2⇒(2a+b)^2-65b^2=±8$ which has no integer solutions $(a,b)$ since ±8 is not quadratic residue mod 5].
    Over $𝐅_3$ this has no roots, so is irreducible, so by Dedekind (3) is inert.
    Since $[𝔭_2]=[𝔮_2]^{-1}$, the class group is generated by $[𝔭_2]$. \begin{align*} 𝔭_2^2&=(4,2α,16+α)&α^2&=16+α \\&=(4,2α,α)&α&=(16+α)-4×4 \\&=(4,α) \\&=(4,α-4) \\&=(α-4)&4&=(α+3)(α-4) \end{align*} So $[𝔭_2]^2=e$. So $\Cl(K)≅C_2$ is generated by $[𝔭_2]$.
  5. Find all integer solutions to the equation $y^2+74=x^3$.
    Let $K=𝐐(\sqrt{-74})$. It turns out that $h_K=10$. In particular, $𝒪_K$ does not have unique factorisation.
    The equation factors in $𝒪_K$ as $(y+\sqrt{-74})(y-\sqrt{-74})=x^3$. We do not have unique factorisation into elements of $𝒪_K$, only into ideals, so we think of this as an equation \[\tag{1} (y+\sqrt{-74})(y-\sqrt{-74})=(x)^3 \] of ideals.
    We are going to prove that the two ideals on the left are coprime. Suppose some prime ideal $𝔭$ divides both terms on the LHS. Then $y+\sqrt{-74}, y-\sqrt{-74} ∈ 𝔭$, and so, taking the difference, $2 \sqrt{-74} ∈ 𝔭$. Therefore $𝔭 ∣(2 \sqrt{-74})$. (Here, we are using the fact that containment and division of ideals are the same thing, Theorem 5.2.)
    Taking norms, we have \[\tag{2} N(𝔭) ∣ N(2 \sqrt{-74})=2^3 ⋅ 37 . \] Also, since $𝔭 ∣(y+\sqrt{-74})$, we have $𝔭 ∣(x)^3$ and so \[\tag{3} N(𝔭) ∣ N\left((x)^3\right)=x^6 . \] We claim that neither 2 nor 37 divides $x$.
    If $2 ∣ x$ then $8 ∣ x^3$, so $y^2=x^3-74 ≡ 2\pmod 4$, a contradiction.
    If $37 ∣ x$ then $37 ∣ y$, and so $37^2 ∣ y^2-x^3=74$. This is also a contradiction.
    From these facts and $(2),(3)$ we have $N(𝔭)=1$, which is impossible; therefore we are forced to conclude that $𝔭$ does not exist, so the ideals $(y+\sqrt{-74}),(y-\sqrt{-74})$ are indeed coprime.

    Now we return to (1). By unique factorisation of ideals, both $(y+\sqrt{-74})$ and $(y-\sqrt{-74})$ are cubes of ideals. Suppose that $(y+\sqrt{-74})=𝔞^3$. In particular, $[𝔞]^3=e$. Since the order of the class group $h_K=10$ is coprime to 3, $[𝔞]$ is trivial, or in other words $𝔞$ is a principal ideal. Thus we have an equation \[ (y+\sqrt{-74})=(a+b \sqrt{-74})^3 \] for some $a, b ∈ 𝐙$. This means that \[ y+\sqrt{-74}=u(a+b \sqrt{-74})^3 \] in $𝒪_K$, where $u$ is a unit. The only units are $± 1$; by replacing $a, b$ with $-a,-b$ if necessary, we may assume that $u=1$.
    Since $\sqrt{-74}$ is irrational, comparing coefficients we obtain \[ y=a(a^2-3×74b^2), b(3 a^2-74b^2)=1. \] The second of these implies that $b= ± 1$ and hence that $3 a^2-74= ± 1$, so $a=±5,y=±197×5=±985,x=99$.
