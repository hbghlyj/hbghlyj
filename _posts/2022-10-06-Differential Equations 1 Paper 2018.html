      1. State Picard’s Theorem on the existence of a unique solution of the differential equation system$$y'(x)=f(x, y),   y(a)=b$$where prime denotes differentiation with respect to $x$.
      2. Let $f(x, y)=x \sqrt{y+1}$. Show that $f$ is not Lipshitz continuous in $y$ on any interval $\abs{y+1}<k$. For $a=0, b=-1$, demonstrate non-uniqueness by constructing two different solutions.
    2. Consider now the differential equation\begin{align} y'(x)&=\tan (y), \\ y(0)&=\fracπ{4} \end{align}
      1. Show that the largest possible value of $h$ for which Picard's Theorem guarantees a unique solution for $\abs{x}<h$ satisfies $$ h=\left[\cos \left(k^*+\fracπ{4}\right)\right]^2, $$ where $k^* ∈(0, π / 4)$ satisfies $$ 2 k^*=\sin \left(2 k^*+\fracπ{2}\right) . $$
      2. By solving (1) analytically, show that in fact a solution can be constructed up to the larger value $h=\ln \sqrt{2}$.
    Solution.
      1. Picard's theorem states that there exists a unique solution to $y'=f,y(a)=b$ on $R=\{\abs{x-a}⩽h,\abs{y-b}⩽k\}$ if
        • $\abs{f(x,y)}<M$ finite on $R$ and $f$ is continuous.
        • $f$ satisfies a Lipschitz condition in $y$ which holds in $R$
        • $Mh⩽k$
      2. \(f = x \sqrt{y + 1}\). \(f\) Lipschitz on \(\abs{y + 1} < k\) would imply \(∃ L\) such that \(\abs{f (x, y) - f (x, z) } < L \abs{y - z}   ∀ y, z\) such that \(\abs{y + 1} < k, \abs{ z + 1 } < k\).
        Let \(z = - 1\). The statement reads \(\abs{x \sqrt{y + 1} } < L \abs{y + 1}  ∀ \abs{y + 1} < k\), which would require \(\frac{\abs{x}}{\sqrt{y + 1}} < L\)
        but then left side blows up as \(y→- 1^+\), so there does not exist such an \(L\). ∴\(f\) not Lipschitz. \begin{cases}y' = x \sqrt{y + 1}\\y (0) = - 1\end{cases} One solution is \(y = - 1\). Can construct a second solution by separating variables: \[\frac{{\text{d}} y}{\sqrt{y + 1}} = x \text{d} x ⇒ 2 \sqrt{y + 1}= \frac{1}{2} x^2 + C, C = 0 \text{ by boundary condition }y (0) = - 1\] \(⇒ y = \frac{1}{16} x^4 - 1\) is a second solution.
      1. $\begin{cases}y' = \tan y\\y (0) =\fracπ{4}\end{cases}$ Since \(\tan y→∞\) as \(y→\fracπ{2}\) must have \(k < \fracπ{4}\).
        For this \(R\), \(M = \tan \left( \fracπ{4} + k \right)\) is a bound on \(f\).
        Also since \(f_y = \sec^2 y\) is bounded on \(R\), \(f\) is Lipschitz, by Mean value theorem.
        Now \(M h ⩽ k ⇒ h ⩽ \frac{k}{\tan \left(\fracπ{4} + k\right)}≕ g (k)\)
        The maximum region is found by seeking a maximum of \(g\) for \(k ∈ \left(0, \fracπ{4} \right)\)
        Since \(g (0) = g \left( \fracπ{4} \right) = 0\), such a maximum does exist. \[g' = \frac{1}{\tan \left( \fracπ{4} + k \right)} - \frac{k}{\tan^2 \left(\fracπ{4} + k \right)} \sec^2 \left( \fracπ{4} + k \right) = 0⇒ k^∗= \sin \left( \fracπ{4} + k^∗\right)\cos\left( \fracπ{4} + k^∗\right)→2 k^∗= \sin\left(\fracπ{2} + 2 k^∗\right)\] And \(h_{\max} = \frac{k^∗}{\tan \left( \fracπ{4} + k^∗\right)} =\left( \cos \left( \fracπ{4} + k^∗\right) \right)^2≈0.163194\)
      2. \[\frac{1}{\tan y} \mathrm{d} y = \frac{\cos y}{\sin y} \mathrm{d} y=\mathrm{d} x⇒\ln \sin y = x + \tilde{c}⇒\sin y =ce^x,   y (0) = \frac{π}{4}⇒\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = c\]So we have the solution \(y = \arcsin \left( \frac{e^x}{\sqrt{2}} \right)\) which is valid when \(\frac{e^x}{\sqrt{2}}≤1⇒x≤\ln \sqrt{2}≈0.346574\)

    1. Consider the Dirichlet problem \begin{align} & u_{x x}+u_{y y}=f(x, y),   x, y∈Ω, \\ & u=g(x, y) \text { on }∂Ω, \end{align} where subscript denotes partial differentiation, and Ω is a simply connected closed region with boundary ∂Ω.
      1. Show that if a solution exists, it is unique. [You may use without proof the Maximum Principle if stated clearly.]
      2. Consider two solutions $u_1$ and $u_2$, both satisfying (3) and with $u_i=g_i(x, y)$ on ∂Ω. If $g_2>g_1$ pointwise on ∂Ω, show that $u_2(x, y)>u_1(x, y) ∀(x, y)∈Ω$.
    2. Consider the partial differential equation system \begin{aligned} & u_{x x}+u_{y y}=0,   y>0 \\ & u(x, 0)=0 \\ & u_y(x, 0)=g(x)=e^{-\sqrt{n}} \cos (n x) \end{aligned} where $n$ is a constant.
      1. Verify that for fixed $n$,$$u(x, y)=\frac{1}{n} e^{-\sqrt{n}} \cos (n x) \sinh(n y)$$is a solution.
      2. By considering the behaviour as $n→∞$, show that the system does not exhibit continuous dependence on data.
    3. Let $w(x,t)$ satisfy \begin{aligned} & w_t=w_{x x}+a w, \text { for } 0< x< 1, t>0 \\ & w=0 \text { at } x=0 \\ & w_x+α w=0 \text { at } x=1, \\ & w=0 \text { at } t=0 \end{aligned} where $a$ and α are both positive constants.
      1. Derive the relation$$\frac{d}{d t} ∫_0^1 \frac12 w^2 d x-∫_0^1 a w^2 d x≤0 .$$
      2. Hence or otherwise show that $w ≡ 0$.
    Solution.
      1. The difference of two solutions $w$ satisfies $∇^2w=0$ on Ω, $w=0$ on ∂Ω.
        Maximum principle: $∇^2u≥0⇒u$ attains maximum on ∂Ω; $∇^2u≤0⇒u$ attains minimum on ∂Ω.
        Since $∇^2w≡0$, $w$ attains max and min on ∂Ω, which is $0⇒w≡0$.
      2. $\cases{∇^2u_1=f\\∇^2u_2=f}$ on Ω and $\cases{u_1=g_1\\u_2=g_2}$ on ∂Ω.
        $w=u_2-u_1$ satisfies $\cases{∇^2w=0\text{ on }Ω\\w=g_2-g_1>0\text{ on }∂Ω}$
        By maximum principle, $w$ attains a minimum on $∂Ω⇒w>0$ on $Ω⇒u_2>u_1$ on Ω
      1. immediate, just plug in.
      2. Let $g_1=0,g_2=e^{-\sqrt n}\cos(nx)$ for which we have solutions $u_1=0,u_2=\frac1ne^{-\sqrt n}\cos(nx)\sinh(ny)$
        As $n→∞$, $g_2→0\;∀x$, so $‖g_1-g_2‖$ can be made arbitrarily small
        But $u_2=\frac1ne^{-\sqrt n}\cos(nx){e^{ny}-e^{-ny}\over2}$ does not go to 0 if $y>0$ and $\cos(nx)≠0$ due to $e^{ny}$ term.
        Hence $‖u_1-u_2‖$ cannot be made arbitrarily small as $n→∞$.
        ∴ no continuous dependence for data.
    3. $\begin{aligned}[t]∂_x(ww_x)&=ww_{xx}+w_x^2\\&=w(w_t-aw)+w_x^2\\&=\frac12∂_t(w^2)-aw^2+w_x^2\end{aligned}$
      Integrating $x$ on $(0,1)$,$$∫_0^1\frac{∂}{∂t}\frac12w^2d x-∫_0^1a w^2d x+∫_0^1w_x^2d x=ww_x|^1_0=-αw(1)^2≤0$$ Hence$$\frac{d}{d t}\frac12∫_0^1w^2d x-a∫_0^1w^2d x≤0$$
    4. Let $I(t)=∫_0^1w^2dx$. Then $I'(t)-2aI(t)≤0$ and $I(0)=0$. Let $ℐ(t)=I(t)e^{-2at}$. Then $ℐ'(t)≤0$ and $ℐ(0)=0⇒ℐ(t)≤0⇒I(t)≤0$
      But $I(t)=∫_0^1w^2dx≥0$, so $I≡0$, so $w≡0$.
